About Me
Professional background and vision
YN

Graduate Student · Instructional Design Program
I am a graduate student specializing in evaluation, assessment, and data-driven learning design. My work sits at the intersection of evidence-based practice and human-centered design, with a commitment to creating learning experiences that are both rigorous and accessible to diverse learners.

My academic background spans curriculum development, educational technology, and learning theory. I am passionate about designing assessment systems that genuinely serve learners — not just measure them — and about using data thoughtfully to inform instructional decisions rather than reduce learners to numbers.
My Vision Statement
"I believe that thoughtful assessment design is at the heart of meaningful learning. My goal is to develop as a practitioner who evaluates not just outcomes, but the quality of learning experiences themselves — and uses that insight to design better ones. I am committed to building a professional practice grounded in reflection, evidence, and a deep respect for the learners I serve."

My Projects
Deliverables developed this semester in evaluation, assessment, and learning design
Here you will find the projects and deliverables I developed this semester. Each entry includes a brief annotation explaining the project's purpose and what competency it demonstrates.
Application Analysis
Phase 1
Platform Evaluation ePortfolio Design Decision Making
A systematic comparative analysis of ePortfolio platforms — Google Sites versus Wix — evaluated against four criteria: ease of use, customization, cost, and integration capability. This project demonstrates my ability to make evidence-based design decisions grounded in audience needs and project constraints, and situates those decisions within the broader literature on ePortfolios as assessment tools.
Sitemap & Storyboard
Phase 2
Information Architecture Visual Design User Experience
A structured planning document presenting the hierarchical sitemap and five-page storyboard for this ePortfolio. The sitemap organizes content thematically by competency domain rather than chronologically, reflecting an audience-centered design logic. Storyboard mock-ups apply Williams and Tollett's alignment, repetition, and contrast principles to create a consistent, professional visual identity.
Develop & Iterate: Usability Testing Report
Phase 3
Usability Testing Iterative Design Formative Assessment
A usability testing report documenting think-aloud sessions with two participants — a course colleague and an HR professional — conducted to evaluate the site's navigability, clarity, and communication of purpose. Findings informed three immediate design revisions and connected usability testing to the formative assessment principles central to this course.

Reflections
Metacognitive thread across the semester
This section runs alongside my project work as a record of my thinking, assumptions, and growth. The portfolios I found most compelling during my exploration were not the most polished — they were the most reflective. That is what I am aiming for here.
Phase 1
Exploration Reflection — Application Analysis
This phase shifted my understanding of ePortfolios from mere digital archives to purposeful, reflective assessment tools. Before engaging with the literature, I understood an ePortfolio loosely as a digital folder where students store completed work. The most significant insight was that reflection — not accumulation — is the engine of portfolio-based learning. The portfolios I found most compelling were not those with the most elaborate designs, but those where the designer's voice, judgment, and growth were audible in every annotation and framing choice. That reorientation changes what I will prioritize as I build: less time curating polished deliverables, more time writing honest, analytical reflections that reveal my thinking process.
Phase 2
Planning Reflection — Sitemap & Storyboard
Creating the sitemap and storyboard revealed that I default to content-first thinking rather than structure-first thinking. The shift from content inventory to user journey was the most important conceptual reframe this phase produced — and one I doubt I would have arrived at if I had simply opened Google Sites and started building pages. I now think of this portfolio not as a showcase but as a structured argument: an artifact that makes a claim about competency and supports that claim with evidence, reflection, and intentional design.
Phase 3
Development Reflection — Usability Testing
The usability testing process revealed a gap I could not have discovered alone: the distance between the designer's fluency and the visitor's first encounter. Watching a participant pause on the word "Assessment" in my navigation and say "I'm not sure what that means here" was genuinely illuminating. This experience connected directly to formative assessment — gathering evidence mid-process to guide improvement rather than rendering a final judgment. It has shifted my understanding of user experience from a design concern to an assessment concern.
